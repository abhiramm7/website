TL;DR

Presents Optimal Hydrograph Separation (OHS), which couples the Eckhardt (2005) Recursive Digital Filter with a Chemical Mass Balance using specific conductance (SC) to objectively optimize the filter parameters α (recession constant) and β (BFImax) without relying on a priori geological classification. Tested at 47 USGS stream sites, the method produces acceptable fits (Nash-Sutcliffe > 0.3, β not at bounds) at 34 sites.

Third pass — critique

Implicit assumptions: - SC perfectly represents a two-component (base flow / surface runoff) mixing system; violations (e.g., multiple groundwater sources, road-salt contamination acknowledged but only partially addressed) would invalidate β estimates. - Linear reservoir assumption (exponential recession) is asserted, not tested against observed recession behavior at each site; if violated, α loses its physical interpretation. - The BFI turning-point factor f = 0.9 is fixed, not optimized; its effect on the recession periods used to estimate α is unexamined. - Median α across recession events is used without justification that the distribution is stable or unimodal.

Missing context or citations: - No engagement with tracers other than SC (chloride, silica, δ¹⁸O) to evaluate whether results are tracer-dependent. - Deterministic modeling and end-member mixing analysis (EMMA) are listed as alternative approaches but never compared to OHS outputs. - No discussion of how anthropogenic SC signals (wastewater effluent, irrigation return flows) affect optimization at the 47 sites.

Possible experimental / analytical issues: - 13 of 47 sites (28%) produce non-unique β solutions; this is a substantial fraction and receives no mechanistic explanation. - Nash-Sutcliffe > 0.3 is a very low acceptance threshold; results at the lower end of this range may not be physically meaningful. - "CS" column in the results table (range 49–388) is never defined; if it is N (block length), values up to 388 days far exceed the literature-standard 5 days and require justification. - No cross-validation, holdout period, or split-sample test; optimized parameters are evaluated on the same record used to fit them. - Uncertainty quantification is stated as a design goal but is absent from reported results. - Non-uniqueness (13 sites) suggests the SC signal may be insufficient to constrain β at many locations, yet this limitation is not discussed as a condition for method applicability.

Ideas for future work: - Conduct split-sample validation: optimize α and β on one half of the record, predict SC on the other, and report out-of-sample RMSE. - Compare OHS-derived base-flow fractions against independent estimates from calibrated groundwater models or environmental tracers (e.g., ³H/³He ages) to establish ground truth. - Quantify sensitivity of β to the choice of CB modeling approach (SCfit vs. sin-cos) and to the fixed BFI parameters (f = 0.9, NR = 3 days). - Investigate whether non-unique sites share identifiable watershed characteristics (geology, land use, SC variability), enabling an a priori flag for method inapplicability.

Claims

  • Base flow is the portion of streamflow sustained between precipitation events via delayed subsurface pathways, and accurate estimation is critical for water management and contaminant assessment.
  • Optimal Hydrograph Separation (OHS) combines a physically-based Recursive Digital Filter with Chemical Mass Balance, enabling objective and reproducible estimation of model parameters alpha and beta.
  • The BFI N-day block width can be optimized by identifying the break point in the piecewise linear relationship between N and long-term average BFI values.
  • Of 47 sites tested, 34 met acceptance criteria (Nash-Sutcliffe efficiency > 0.3 and optimized beta), with OHS yielding base-flow index estimates comparable to standard BFI results.
  • The recession constant alpha can be objectively estimated from streamflow records using periods where BFI-identified base flow equals total streamflow.
